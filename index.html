1. Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement & Background

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) is one of the most critical obstetric emergencies in modern medicine, defined as blood loss exceeding 500 mL following a vaginal delivery or 1,000 mL following a caesarean section. It remains the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, accounting for approximately 27% of all maternal deaths globally, translating to roughly 14 million cases and 70,000 deaths annually (Say et al., 2014; WHO, 2023). The condition can deteriorate rapidly, with patients losing upwards of 1,000 mL of blood before displaying visible signs of haemodynamic instability, leaving clinicians with an extremely narrow window for effective intervention (Rath, 2021). In Singapore, while maternal mortality rates are significantly lower than global averages, PPH remains a leading cause of maternal morbidity, with severe cases resulting in ICU admissions, emergency hysterectomies, and long-term psychological trauma (Chauke et al., 2023).

Despite its clinical significance, the high-acuity, low-frequency nature of PPH means that medical students in Singapore rarely have the opportunity to engage actively in its management during clinical rotations. Because these events are life-threatening and demand immediate senior-level intervention, students are ethically and practically relegated to the role of passive observers. They may witness a PPH resuscitation in the room, but are seldom given the opportunity to lead, or even participate in, the decision-making process. This creates a significant theory-practice gap, where students possess the physiological knowledge of PPH management but lack the environmental exposure required to execute these protocols confidently under intense pressure.

Current training modalities, primarily consisting of didactic lectures and scheduled manikin-based simulations, fail to bridge this gap effectively. Lectures provide theoretical grounding but lack the sensory immersion needed to prepare students for the visual shock of rapid blood loss and the psychological pressure of a deteriorating patient. Manikin-based simulations, while more hands-on, are limited by their high cost, resource intensity, and logistical complexity, meaning students often receive only one or two practice opportunities per academic year. This frequency is far below the threshold required for procedural confidence to become reflexive. Furthermore, traditional group simulations often suffer from the "observer effect," where only one student takes the lead while peers watch passively, resulting in inconsistent skill acquisition across the cohort.

Without sufficient hands-on exposure, students enter their housemanship with theoretical confidence but practical hesitation. When confronted with a real PPH in a labor ward, this hesitation can manifest as fixation errors, delayed escalation, or an inability to prioritise interventions under pressure. Consequently, there is an urgent need for a training solution that provides a high-fidelity, risk-free environment where students can move beyond passive observation and develop the procedural competence and clinical reflexes required to manage a postpartum hemorrhage effectively.

1.2 Novelty and Value Proposition

To address the training gaps identified in current obstetric education, this project proposes the development of a Virtual Reality (VR) simulation specifically designed for PPH management training. Built on the Meta Quest 3 headset and developed in Unity, the simulation places the learner inside a virtual NUH labor ward where they must independently manage a deteriorating postpartum patient through a structured sequence of clinical interventions. This directly tackles the core problem: that medical students currently have no safe, repeatable, and hands-on platform to practice PPH management before entering the real ward.

The novelty of this solution lies in its ability to transcend the limitations of traditional manikin-based simulation. Manikins are inherently limited by their static nature and high logistical requirements, meaning students receive little more than one or two practice opportunities per year. In contrast, the Meta Quest 3 is portable and standalone, allowing a student to engage in a high-intensity PPH drill multiple times in a single session without any additional infrastructure. This enables the high-frequency repetition of procedural sequences until actions become reflexive, functioning as a flight simulator for obstetric emergencies and ensuring that a student's first experience managing a life-threatening bleed is not on a live patient (Ericsson, 2004).

By concentrating development efforts on procedural accuracy and sequential clinical workflow, this simulation provides a robust and scalable bridge between theoretical classroom knowledge and the high-stakes reality of the delivery suite.

1.3 Scope Refinement following Interim Assessment

Following our interim project demonstration and clinician feedback, we refined the project scope to better align with the Final Year Project timeline. While our initial proposal included environmental realism, empathy training, and voice recognition, the complexity of developing natural language processing and conversational branching proved unfeasible within the timeframe. Attempting to integrate all three elements risked compromising the stability of the core simulation.

Consequently, we pivoted from an open-ended, highly realistic environment to a structured, guided sequence focused on clinical management. This ensures that learners move through the correct steps in the right order, helping them build a strong, systematic approach to decision-making without the added complexity of open-ended interactions. While this is more constrained than what we initially envisioned, it allowed us to focus our efforts on getting the clinical workflow right, ensuring the system runs reliably, and providing meaningful feedback to users.

This refinement in scope allowed us to come up with a more streamlined and reliable learning instrument, that prioritises on building core procedural skills and clinical sequencing first, thus creating a solid foundation that future iterations can build on, such as the ones discussed in Section 7.3.

2. Design Strategy

2.1 Identified Training Gaps

Our research and discussions with Dr. Arundhati revealed two primary reasons traditional medical education fails to prepare students for real-world Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) emergencies. The first relates to cognitive load. In an actual PPH scenario, students are expected to do multiple things at once, such as monitoring rapidly changing vital signs, recalling drug dosages, and carrying out clinical interventions, all under significant time pressure. This level of multitasking is difficult to replicate in lectures or occasional manikin-based sessions. Dr. Arundhati also highlighted that cognitive freezing is commonly seen in junior doctors when they face their first obstetric emergency, which reinforces this gap.

The second issue is the development of motor skills. Key tasks such as setting up IV access, performing uterine massage, and administering uterotonics in the correct sequence require hands-on repetition to build muscle memory. However, with formal PPH training typically taking place only twice a year, students simply do not get enough exposure for these skills to become instinctive. Taken together, these gaps point to the need for a training approach that brings both cognitive pressure and procedural practice into one accessible platform, which this VR simulation is designed to provide.

2.2 Subject Matter Research

The development of this VR training module was an iterative process driven by continuous professional oversight and evidence-based medical research. The primary objective was to ensure that every virtual interaction, from identifying a risk factor to administering medication mirrors real-world high-stakes obstetrics.

2.2.1 Doctor Consultations

The primary clinical advisor for this project is Dr Arundhati from the National University of Singapore (NUH). Our team engaged in a consistent consultation schedule, initially meeting on a weekly and biweekly basis, and later transitioning to monthly sessions as the development progressed. These consultations were critical for validating the technical and medical accuracy of the simulation.

Dr Arundhati provided essential resources, as shown in figures 1 to 3, including a specialised PPH textbook, which served as the primary medical reference for building the scenario's logic. This text allowed the team to move beyond the general medical knowledge and incorporate specific, evidence-based metrics, such as the relationship between material hemoglobin levels and physiological reserve. These expert sessions highlighted a critical training gap: while medical students are often proficient in theoretical management protocols, they lack the experiential exposure to manage a patient who is rapidly deteriorating. Consequently, the research focused on how to translate Dr Arundhati's real-world expertise into guided clinical reasoning prompts that mirror the mentorship a junior doctor would receive in a real delivery ward.

2.2.2 Clinical Workflow and Scenario Logic

The clinical workflow developed for this simulation is structured to guide the learner through a realistic progression of PPH management, transitioning from routine postpartum monitoring to active crisis response. By using a deteriorating scenario, learners start with a stable patient before identifying emerging signs of postpartum hemorrhage. To ground the scenario in reality, the patient profile includes specific risk factors like maternal anemia and fetal macrosomia, teaching students that PPH is often predictable rather than a random event.

Once the crisis begins, the simulation follows a structured emergency protocol, covering everything from bleeding recognition and IV access to blood tests and drug administration. To reinforce systematic care, the workflow is sequential, requiring one step to be mastered before the next unlocks. The simulation also incorporates physiological indicators such as heart rate and blood pressure changing dynamically as the scenario progresses. Learners are required to perform both procedural tasks and clinical decision-making simultaneously, ensuring the experience addresses technical competence and analytical reasoning in equal measure.

This process ensured that the final technical product was not merely a digital environment, but a targeted educational intervention designed to address the specific cognitive deficiencies and procedural hesitations of medical students. By focusing on the user experience through a structured persona, the development remained anchored in the reality of the learner's transition from theoretical knowledge to clinical application.

Phase 1: Persona Development and Problem Scaffolding

This early phase of design concentrated on developing "John," a Year 4 medical student persona. We identified that even though one student has enough theoretical information to cope with standard tests, he does not have proper psychological readiness and clinical courage to become the leader of PPH resuscitation procedure. With the help of John's academic journey, we discovered that the substantial problem was not a lack of access to medical textbooks but a lack of such a learning environment where he could practice making decisions without consequences. This persona-driven research highlighted that the VR system needed to prioritise a sense of responsibility and procedural flow to effectively bridge the gap between a student's classroom confidence and their real-world clinical performance.

3. VR System Development

3.1 System Architecture & Technical Overview

The system architecture of the Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) VR Simulation is designed as a modular, event-driven framework optimised for high-fidelity clinical training on the Meta Quest 3 headset. The architecture is bifurcated into three primary layers: the Hardware/Driver Layer, the Middleware/SDK Layer, and the Application Logic Layer, ensuring a clear separation of concerns between the physical input and clinical consequences.

At the Hardware Layer, the system utilises the Quest 3's advanced sensors for 6 Degrees-of-Freedom (6-DoF) tracking and high-resolution passthrough. This layer communicates with the Middleware Layer via the OpenXR standard. By utilising the Meta XR Core SDK and Interaction SDK, the system translates raw sensor data into actionable "Interactors." This architectural decision allows the simulation to process complex hand-tracking data such as "pinch" and "poke" gestures and map them to virtual medical instruments with sub-millimeter precision.

Figure 11: OVR Camera and Hands Hierarchy Setup

The Application Logic Layer is governed by a Centralised State Machine designed to handle the non-linear nature of medical emergencies. Unlike traditional linear software, this simulation utilises a Sequence Manager (implemented via the ButtonSequencer class) which leverages C# Asynchronous Coroutines. This allows the architecture to handle time-dependent clinical events such as the 10-second spreading of the StainedSheet or the staggered activation of NurseAudio without freezing the main execution thread. By using IEnumerator functions, the system can "yield" execution, effectively creating a "living" medical environment where vitals (e.g., slowBeat vs. fastBeat) and visual cues change dynamically based on the user's progress.

A critical component of this architecture is the Interaction Wrapper and Event System. By employing the PointableUnityEventWrapper, the system decouples the physical interaction from the logic. When a user interacts with a Syringe, the architecture does not just trigger a static response; it queries a Validation Layer (the TrayItemChecker). This layer compares the user's action against a predefined procedural array (objectHighlighters and placementGuides). If the interaction matches the expected clinical step, the system updates the global state; if not, it triggers a visual "Glow" feedback loop via the GlowObjectCmd system.

Furthermore, the architecture includes a Spatial UI Subsystem. Because medical simulations require constant data monitoring, the system implements a dynamic UI placement logic. Using the SyringeCollisionPopup script, the architecture calculates the contact point between the Syringe and the MotherBody tag, then uses a Billboarding Algorithm to ensure the feedback canvas always faces the user's MainCamera. This ensures that critical instructional information is never lost in the 3D environment, regardless of the user's physical orientation in the room.

Finally, the data flow is optimised for the mobile chipset of the Quest 3 by utilising the Vulkan Graphics API. This architectural choice supports advanced rendering features like "Optimise Buffer Discards," which maintain a high frame rate (72Hz+) even when the simulation is managing multiple concurrent scripts, 3D audio spatialisation, and dynamic lighting for the emergency room environment. This robust architecture ensures that the simulation remains a reliable, immersive, and pedagogically sound tool for emergency obstetric training.

3.3 VR Scenario Design

The VR scenario is meticulously structured into five chronological stages that mirror the high-stakes environment of a delivery ward. Each scene is designed to move the learner from passive observation to active clinical intervention, using interactive quizzes and decision points to ensure that the student's mental processing is aligned with the physical actions required to save a life.

Scene 0: Scenario Introduction (Context)

The simulation commences in a relatively calm environment to establish a baseline. The learner is introduced to the patient, a 35-year-old female who has just completed a normal vaginal delivery. The primary goal of this scene is to test the student's ability to identify red flags before they escalate into an emergency.

Interactive Logic: The system presents a patient profile UI. The learner must review the admission Hb of 9.6 g/dL and the newborn's weight of 3.9kg.

The Quiz Trigger: A pop-up asks the student to identify if the patient is at high risk for PPH. This is a critical check. The clinical reasoning provided in the simulation explains that an Hb < 11 g/dL indicates anemia, which significantly reduces the patient's physiological reserve. By forcing the student to acknowledge these factors (Macrosomia and Anemia) through a "Yes/No" submission, the simulation primes them to act fast once the bleeding begins.

Figure 12: Scene 0 quiz prompt assessing learner's identification of PPH risk factors

Scene 1: Emergency Recognition (Sensory Triggering)

In this scene, the simulation shifts from data review to crisis recognition. The transition is triggered by an auditory alert from the midwife: "Doctor, she's soaked two sheets already!" This is accompanied by the visual rendering of blood spreading on the delivery bed.

The Task: The student must perform a Visual Estimation of Blood Loss (VEBL). In traditional training, blood loss is often underestimated. To combat this, the VR environment requires the student to interact with the blood-soaked linen and submit an estimate.

The Quiz Trigger: A multiple-choice prompt asks the learner to estimate the volume (e.g., 200ml, 500ml, 800ml and 1000ml). Selecting 800ml/ 1000ml is the only way to progress, reinforcing the clinical standard that any loss over 500ml constitutes an emergency. This scene teaches the student to trust clinical signs over subjective observation.

Figure 13: Scene 1 quiz prompt assessing learner visual estimation of blood loss

Scene 2: Medical Intervention (Intravenous Access)

In this scene, the simulation transitions from the identification of the crisis to the initial phase of medical stabilisation. The urgency of the situation is reinforced by the patient's deteriorating vitals on the monitor, and the student must prioritise establishing a reliable circulatory route. This stage is designed to address the motor skill deficiency in selecting and preparing high-stakes medical equipment under time pressure.

The Task: The student must interact with the medical trolley to select the appropriate Intravenous (IV) equipment for fluid resuscitation. This involves identifying the correct gauge of the cannula required for emergency volume replacement. In a real-world PPH scenario, the speed and bore-size of the IV access can determine the success of subsequent fluid and blood transfusions. The VR environment requires the student to physically pick up the cannula and prepare for insertion.

The Quiz Trigger: A multiple-choice prompt pauses the action and asks the learner: "Which size of IV cannula is most appropriate for a patient experiencing PPH?" with options including 22G, 20G, and 16G/18G. Selecting the 16G or 18G large-bore cannula is the only way to progress. This reinforces the clinical logic that large-bore access is mandatory for rapid resuscitation. The scene teaches the student that procedural precision is just as vital as diagnostic speed during an obstetric emergency.

Figure 14: Scene 2 quiz prompt assessing learner identification of correct gauge of the cannula required

Scene 3: Medical Intervention (Blood Test and Laboratory Analysis)

In this scene, the simulation moves from the physical setup of the IV to the essential task of diagnostic quantification. While the IV access allows for the administration of fluids and medications, a formal blood test is required to assess the patient's current physiological state and prepare for potential deterioration. This stage is designed to teach the student that physical observation must be supported by accurate laboratory data to provide a comprehensive clinical picture.

The Task: The student must utilise the previously established large-bore IV line to draw a series of blood samples. In the VR environment, this requires a specific sequence of actions: selecting the correct color-coded collection tubes from the medical trolley. This reinforces the procedural accuracy required to ensure that the laboratory receives the correct samples for urgent processing, as errors in tube selection can lead to critical delays in a high-stress ward environment.

The Quiz Trigger: A multiple-choice prompt pauses the action and asks the learner: "Which of the following blood tests should be ordered for a patient with PPH?" with options including Full Blood Count (FBC), Group and Cross-match, Coagulation Profile, and Renal/Liver Function tests. Selecting "All of the above" is the only way to progress. This reinforces the clinical protocol that a comprehensive panel is necessary to monitor Hemoglobin levels, prepare for blood transfusions, and check for clotting deficiencies (Thrombin) simultaneously.

Figure 15: Scene 3 quiz prompt assessing learner identification of correct color-coded collection tubes

Scene 4: Medical Intervention (Uterotonics Administration)

In this scene, the simulation progresses from diagnostic preparation to active hemorrhage control. Following the establishment of IV access and completion of blood investigations, the patient continues to exhibit moderate bleeding with signs of physiological compensation (tachycardia with relatively stable blood pressure). This stage emphasises timely pharmacological intervention alongside physical techniques to promote uterine contraction and reduce ongoing blood loss. It reinforces that postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) management requires both mechanical (uterine massage) and medical (uterotonics) approaches simultaneously.

The Task: The student is first prompted by the nurse to initiate uterine massage, supported by an on-screen animation that the learner must follow. This reinforces immediate first-line management for uterine atony. Subsequently, a pop-up appears stating "Start medication," revealing a tray of drugs including Oxytocin, Misoprostol, Tranexamic acid, and Carboprost. The learner must correctly sequence these medications based on standard PPH management protocols. This step ensures that the student understands prioritisation in drug administration rather than random selection, reflecting real-life urgency and decision-making under pressure.

The Quiz Trigger: A sequencing-based interaction requires the learner to arrange the medications in the correct order. Incorrect sequencing prevents progression, reinforcing clinical reasoning in pharmacological management. This aligns with the protocol that Oxytocin is typically first-line, followed by additional uterotonics and adjuncts if bleeding persists. The action then transitions into an animation of the nurse administering the medications, visually linking decision-making to intervention.

Figure 16: Scene 4 interactive quiz prompt assessing learner identification of correct order of medications

When the right steps are taken, the environment gives instant feedback: vital signs improve (heart rate drops to about 90 bpm and blood pressure stabilizes around 108/70), and bleeding is clearly reduced (less blood on the sheets). "Bleeding is starting to slow, but she still needs close monitoring," the nurse continues, reiterating that continued attention is necessary even when the initial crisis is under control. Finally, a confirmation pop-up appears: "You successfully managed the PPH situation!", marking the completion of this intervention phase while emphasising clinical responsibility beyond immediate stabilisation.

3.4 Core Functionalities

This section outlines the core functionalities of the VR-based postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) training system, focusing on three key components: interaction system, decision-making points, and feedback system. These components are designed to simulate real clinical workflows while promoting active learning, critical thinking, and procedural accuracy. By integrating these elements, the system ensures that learners are not passive observers but active participants in patient management.

3.4.1 Interaction System

The interaction system defines how learners physically engage with the virtual clinical environment. Designed using a guided point-and-click approach, it balances usability with realistic hospital workflows:

  • Diagnostic Interactions: Learners interact with the patient and monitors to assess clinical status. Clicking the monitor displays vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure), while interacting with the soaked bedsheet allows learners to estimate blood loss volume.
  • Procedural Interactions: The system simulates real-world clinical tasks, such as selecting the appropriate cannula size and inserting an IV line. The system enforces strict task order, blocking progression until essential steps are completed.
  • Multimodal Tasks: In later scenes, interactions become complex. Learners must perform uterine massage by following guided animations while simultaneously interacting with a medication tray to administer uterotonics.
  • Usability Aids: To manage cognitive load, the system utilises pop-up instructions, allowing the learner to focus on clinical decisions rather than struggling with VR navigation.

Figure 17: Pop-up guiding the learner to the next task

Figure 18: Soaked blood sheet & Interactive patient monitor displaying the vitals

3.4.2 Decision-Making Points

Decision-making points are strategically embedded to develop clinical reasoning and prioritisation skills under time-sensitive conditions:

  • Diagnostic Thresholds: Learners must visually estimate blood loss and apply clinical thresholds to officially classify the situation as a PPH, determining the urgency of the intervention.
  • Treatment Selection: Learners must justify their actions, such as selecting a large-bore cannula (16G/18G) for rapid fluid resuscitation, or ordering a comprehensive panel of blood investigations (e.g., FBC, coagulation profile, crossmatch).
  • Pharmacological Sequencing: Learners are presented with a tray of uterotonic medications (oxytocin, misoprostol, tranexamic acid, carboprost) and must arrange them in the correct clinical order. Incorrect sequencing prevents progression.
  • Error-Based Learning: Incorrect decisions do not instantly terminate the simulation. Instead, learners are permitted multiple attempts, building confidence through reflective trial-and-error.

3.4.3 Feedback System

The system provides immediate, continuous, and meaningful responses to learner actions, combining visual, verbal, and physiological feedback:

  • Immediate Task Feedback: Correct actions trigger green highlights and positive verbal confirmation from the virtual nurse. Incorrect actions trigger red highlights and corrective audio prompts.
  • Real-Time Physiological Response: As the learner successfully administers treatments, the patient's condition dynamically improves, visible blood loss reduces, heart rate decreases, and blood pressure stabilises. This establishes a clear cause-and-effect relationship for the student.
  • Immersive Audio Guidance: The virtual nurse simulates clinical teamwork by providing verbal updates on patient status and reminding the learner to act quickly, maintaining a sense of urgency without breaking immersion.
  • Summative Debriefing: Upon successful stabilisation, a confirmation message appears. This is followed by a scaffolding reduction (where guidance fades as the learner progresses) and a final review phase where learners can reflect on their clinical performances.

4. Technical Implementation & Simulation Design

4.1 Environment design

The environmental design of the VR simulation was guided by the clinical authenticity concept, which ensured that the virtual delivery ward is sufficiently representative of a real labor ward environment to facilitate procedural instruction. While visual fidelity was not the project's primary goal, it was critical that the environment seemed familiar and contextually appropriate, allowing learners to engage with the clinical scenario without being distracted.

The virtual ward was created using Unity's 3D workspace, and the spatial arrangement of medical equipment was designed to mimic a typical delivery room layout. The patient bed is the major focus point, flanked by IV stands, medication trolleys, and physiological monitors placed in therapeutically logical areas. This purposeful layout compels the learner to physically turn and locate equipment within the area, developing fundamental situational awareness as they go through the scenario. Lighting was set to be clinical and bright, creating a calm baseline setting that changes tone as the emergency condition is activated.

The scene includes 3D assets such as a patient model, detailed medical hardware including varied sized cannulas, and specific uterotonic vials on the medication tray. These elements were included not for decorative purposes but to ensure that the procedural interactions within the simulation, such as selecting the correct cannula size or identifying the right medication, are grounded in a recognisable clinical context.

Figure 24, 25, 26: Overview of the virtual delivery ward environment

4.2 Prototype Walkthrough

The VR-based postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) simulation's entire user experience is described in the prototype walkthrough. From routine postpartum monitoring to emergency care and stabilization, the system is intended to lead the student through a realistic clinical scenario. As each step reflects actual workflows, the student can actively participate in patient care while responding to a scenario that is always changing.

Stage 1: Entry into the Clinical Environment

The simulation begins with the learner entering a virtual delivery ward. The patient is positioned on the bed, accompanied by the partner, creating a realistic postpartum environment. Medical equipment such as the vital signs monitor, IV stand, and medication trolley are visible within the surrounding.

At this stage, the environment appears stable, allowing the learner to familiarise themselves with the surroundings. The monitor displays baseline vital signs, and no immediate action is required. This initial phase serves as an orientation period, helping the learner understand the layout of the room and identify key equipment.

Stage 2: Recognition of Clinical Deterioration

The scenario escalates as the patient begins to show signs of deterioration. The vital signs monitor emits rapid beeping sounds, indicating an increase in heart rate. The nurse provides a verbal prompt, alerting the learner that the patient has soaked multiple sheets.

The learner is required to assess the patient's condition by interacting with the monitor. After selecting, vital signs are displayed, displaying tachycardia and a drop in blood pressure.

To better assess the situation, the learner interacts with the soaked sheets. A prompt appears, requiring selection of the estimated blood loss volume. Choosing the correct range confirms that the patient is experiencing postpartum hemorrhage. This moment marks the transition from routine monitoring to emergency response, emphasising the importance of early recognition.

Stage 3: Immediate Intervention - IV Access

Following the diagnosis of PPH, the learner is prompted to initiate immediate intervention. The nurse indicates the need for IV access.

A selection interface presents different cannula sizes. The learner must choose the appropriate one to proceed. If an incorrect option is selected, feedback is provided, prompting the learner to reassess their choice.

Once the correct cannula is selected, the learner inserts the IV line by interacting with the patient's arm. An animation confirms the successful placement. The learner then activates IV fluids through an on-screen prompt. This stage highlights the urgency of stabilising the patient's circulation.

Stage 4: Diagnostic Evaluation - Blood Investigations

With IV access established, the learner proceeds to order blood investigations. A prompt appears, instructing the learner to select relevant laboratory tests.

Multiple options are displayed, including full blood count, coagulation profile, renal function tests, liver function tests, and blood group with crossmatch. The learner must select all appropriate investigations to continue. This step reinforces the importance of comprehensive assessment in managing PPH.

After the correct selections are made, the nurse collects the blood samples and sends them to the laboratory. This action is represented through a brief animation, maintaining continuity within the simulation. The learner is reminded that diagnostic results will support further management decisions.

Stage 5: Active Management - Uterine Massage

As the patient continues to bleed, the scenario progresses into active management. The nurse prompts the learner to perform uterine massage, a key first-line intervention for uterine atony.

An animation appears to guide the learner through the correct technique. The learner follows the visual cues to perform the action, reinforcing procedural understanding. This stage emphasises the importance of immediate physical intervention alongside medical treatment.

Stage 6: Pharmacological Intervention - Uterotonics

Following uterine massage, the learner is prompted to initiate pharmacological treatment. A medication tray appears, displaying uterotonic drugs including oxytocin, misoprostol, tranexamic acid, and carboprost.

The learner must arrange these medications in the correct order of administration. This sequencing task reflects real clinical practice, where treatments are prioritised based on effectiveness and urgency. Incorrect sequencing prevents progression, requiring the learner to reconsider their choices.

Once the correct sequence is established, an animation shows the nurse administering the medications. This transition from decision-making to action reinforces the connection between clinical reasoning and intervention.

Stage 7: Patient Response and Stabilisation

Following successful intervention, the simulation provides immediate feedback through changes in the patient's condition. Visual indicators show a reduction in bleeding, with less blood visible on the sheets. The environment becomes less chaotic, reflecting improved control of the situation.

Simultaneously, the patient's vital signs begin to stabilise. The heart rate decreases, and blood pressure returns to a safer range. These changes demonstrate the effectiveness of the learner's actions, reinforcing the relationship between timely intervention and positive outcomes.

The nurse provides verbal feedback, stating that the bleeding is slowing but continued monitoring is required. This emphasises that while the acute crisis is managed, patient care must continue.

Stage 8: Scenario Completion and Outcome Feedback

Once the patient is stabilised, the simulation presents a confirmation message indicating successful management of the PPH scenario. This marks the completion of the active intervention phase.

The message serves as both feedback and motivation, reinforcing the learner's achievement. It also signals the transition from active management to reflection and learning consolidation.

Overall User Experience

The prototype walkthrough demonstrates a cohesive and immersive learning experience, where the learner progresses through a realistic clinical scenario in a structured and engaging manner. Each stage builds upon the previous one, reinforcing both procedural skills and clinical reasoning.

By integrating interactive tasks, real-time feedback, and progressive scenario development, the simulation enables learners to actively participate in managing postpartum hemorrhage. This approach enhances engagement, improves knowledge retention, and prepares learners for real-world clinical situations.

5. VR Development Iteration

5.1 Iterative Development: Initial to Refined Prototype

The development process for the VR PPH simulation involved an iterative prototyping approach, in which a number of design-build-test phases were executed. The development process involved addressing specific challenges, moving from the initial broad story model design to a more refined procedural trainer design. This change was necessary due to the reduction of cognitive load while increasing the depth and accuracy of clinical interaction.

5.1.1 Initial Prototype: A Comprehensive Clinical Journey

The initial prototyping phase also known as interim prototype (before redirection of scope) was guided by five key development objectives: VR storyboarding, delivery room environment construction, PPH patient avatar, dynamic audio integration, and empathy role-play training. These shaped a series of four iterative builds, each targeting a distinct technical problem from establishing interactive UI and AI character animation (Prototypes 1 & 2), to solving event sequencing via a centralised "Director" script (Prototype 3), and testing visual and audio realism alongside conversational AI (Prototype 4).

5.2 Strategic Scope Refinement

Following the interim presentation and clinical consultations, the project underwent a strategic scope refinement. The empathy and conversational AI components, while promising in concept, introduced a level of complexity that could not be adequately validated within the remaining development window. Attempting to manage both soft-skill acquisition and complex clinical algorithms within a single VR session created unnecessary cognitive friction and diluted the technical rigour required for the simulation's core purpose.

The decision was made to narrow the project's focus by removing the empathy and communication components, allowing engineering efforts to be fully redirected towards procedural flow accuracy and the assessment of learners' clinical knowledge at key decision points throughout the PPH management sequence. The simulation had always centred on uterine atony as the primary clinical pathway, and this refinement ensured that the depth and rigour of that core experience was not compromised by overextension into soft-skill training.

The fragmented nodes have been reconstructed to reflect four clinical stages: Recognition, Immediate Actions, Intervention, and Reevaluation as seen in the improved flowchart (Figure 27). 6. Preliminary User Testing

6.1 Methodology

This study adopts a mixed-methods approach, integrating both quantitative and qualitative techniques to evaluate the usability and educational effectiveness of the VR simulation developed for PPH training. This approach enables a comprehensive assessment by combining measurable usability outcomes with in-depth expert insights on clinical and pedagogical relevance.

6.1.1 Quantitative Approach

A mixed method approach will be used in this investigation, where the VR simulation for PPH education is assessed through the application of both quantitative and qualitative methods to determine its usability and effectiveness as a learning tool.

For the quantification part of the investigation, the System Usability Scale(SUS) will be applied. This system is extensively validated and used to evaluate the usability of systems, especially within the context of healthcare technologies. The questionnaire comprises 10 items, which are rated on a five-point Likert scale, from 1 (Strongly disagree) to 5 (Strongly agree). Important factors considered in the system include system usage, user confidence, and the integration of various features within the system. Examples of statements in this scale are as follows: Example items include "I found the system easy to use," "I felt confident using the system," and "I found the various functions in this system were well integrated."

Note: Please refer to Figure 18 in the Appendix for the detailed SUS Questionnaire.

The participants, consisting of 8-10 students, will conduct the SUS test following their engagement with the VR simulation. This sample size is ideal for usability testing, given that the purpose here is to establish trends about usability rather than make generalisations about the population. The SUS has been chosen due to its ease of administration and capacity to provide a usability score against established benchmarks.

SUS scores are rated between 0 and 100, whereby 68 represents average usability. Those above this value reflect high usability, and this is key for this virtual reality simulation as poor usability could impede the learning objectives, especially regarding how one needs to proceed to handle a PPH situation.

Quantitative analysis will involve calculating the mean SUS score, standard deviation, and individual item responses to identify usability strengths and weaknesses. These results will support comparisons across iterations of the prototype and guide improvements.

6.1.2 Qualitative Approach: Expert Clinical Review

In addition to quantitatively analysing user responses, a qualitative analysis was undertaken through a professional clinical review. Dr. Arundhati, a practising obstetrician who has experience in dealing with cases of PPH due to uterine atony. After using the final VR prototype, she gave her answers to a set of open-ended questions:

  1. "Is the sequence of events clinically accurate for uterine atony-induced PPH?"
  2. "Are the decision points realistic for junior doctors or medical students?"
  3. "Would this simulation be useful for training novice learners?"

Her responses were recorded and analysed to identify key insights regarding clinical accuracy, realism, and educational value, allowing for a deeper understanding of how well the simulation aligns with real-world clinical practice.

Her qualitative insights also surfaced a set of forward-looking recommendations, spanning scene realism, pharmacological completeness, interaction design, and audio integration. The following recommendations provide more context than what can be gathered from quantitative metrics like the SUS.

6.1.3 Mixed-Methods Rationale and Importance

Quantitative and qualitative methodological approaches are used together for a comprehensive assessment of the VR simulation. On one hand, the SUS allows gathering some objective data about its usability, and on the other hand, an expert clinical analysis reveals more about the clinical accuracy and educational relevance of the simulation. This strengthens the overall validity of the evaluation.

In VR medical training, both usability and accuracy are crucial factors as low usability will decrease engagement, and incorrect content will negatively affect the educational value of the solution. This is why an approach that focuses on assessing both of the factors is essential for making sure that the core goals of the simulation are achieved, namely increasing the user's confidence and providing a good understanding of how to handle PPH. The analysis combines SUS benchmarking with thematic findings from Dr. Arundhati's review, with future improvements guided by the usability issues and expert recommendations identified through this process.

6.2 Expert Clinical Evaluation

Expert clinical evaluation plays an essential part in verifying any simulation tool designed for training purposes because it makes sure that it meets the requirements of a professional training environment and is consistent with actual clinical practice. For these reasons, the VR simulation prototype was evaluated by Dr. Arundhati, an active obstetrician, experienced in the treatment of post-partum hemorrhage. The expert clinical evaluation was based on a direct trial of the VR prototype, where Dr. Arundhati gave her feedback based on her clinical expertise. These results will be discussed below with respect to strengths and future recommendations.

6.2.1 Strengths Identified

Dr. Arundhati had positive comments on the overall effectiveness of the simulation exercise. The delivery room scene was highly appreciated as realistic and excellent, affirming that the spatial layout and clinical equipment are sufficiently representative of an actual obstetric setting which is a foundational requirement for effective immersive training. Additionally, the clinical scenario was considered as elaborate and accurate as Dr. Arundhati assured that the steps depicted in the scenario follow the proper steps in PPH management, which is the goal of the simulation exercise. She further acknowledged the level of development achieved within a short timeframe.

6.2.2 Areas Requiring Improvement

Scene Realism

The following shortcomings were observed with regard to the behaviour of characters and progression of emergencies. She offered suggestions that the realism element in this simulation needs to be improved further in the future. The husband character can be represented as standing and panicked since such was the level of emotion involved in this situation of an obstetric emergency. Another suggestion was for more midwives to enter the room to reflect the multi-disciplinary team response expected in practice.

Pharmacological Completeness

Dr. Arundhati affirmed that the simulation currently contains important uterotonic drugs, and suggested that in subsequent simulations, the drug trolley should be updated to contain injections of Syntocinon and Ergometrine, in addition to other drugs contained in the drug trolley. The inclusion of these drugs together with the ones already in the drug trolley will increase the realism of the simulation exercise.

Blood Volume and Haematological Investigations

According to Dr. Arundhati, the amount of blood present in the scenario could be made higher for subsequent simulations to emphasise the clinical importance. The increased visualisation of the amount of blood loss would better communicate the critical nature of the case to the learners. She further stated that incorporating the process of collecting the blood sample and conducting a group and cross-matching test would be a useful feature for future simulations. Although not an essential part of the clinical pathway, she believed that it would enhance the learning process for the learners.

Pictorial Blood Loss Estimation Aid

Dr. Arundhati recommended a visual chart estimating blood loss to be added as a feedback pop-up after the question on blood loss estimation. Since this is common practice in clinical practice, she highlighted that adding this feature would enhance the training even more by providing learners with a real-life evidence-based skill related to PPH management.

Usability: Colour Contrast and Audio

Dr. Arundhati suggested two usability recommendations for its future development. The first recommendation is to introduce color coding between the questions and answers in order to enhance readability and reduce cognitive complexity in the visually demanding VR environment. The second recommendation was to incorporate ambient audio into the future iterations of the simulation, which may include clinical alarms, sounds from monitors, conversations among hospital staff, and instructional audio cues.

6.2.3 Summary

According to Dr. Arundhati's assessment, the simulation created a solid and credible clinical platform with a realistic environment, accurate steps involved, and an overall good execution. Her observations were quite positive and future-oriented, providing a number of suggestions for further improvement of the simulation in its subsequent versions. Some of these include improving the believability of the scene by making characters more interactive, adding extra uterotonics such as Syntocinon and Ergometrine to the drug trolley, and finding ways of making interactions more user-friendly through such features as color contrast, audio addition, and an image-based method for estimating blood loss. Moreover, she suggested adding more visibility of the blood volume and conducting a group and cross-match test to improve the clinical aspect of the simulation.

7. Discussions and Recommendations

7.1 Project Limitations

Despite the potential of Virtual Reality (VR) as an innovative educational tool for Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) training, this project has several limitations that should be acknowledged.

S/N Limitation Description Impact on Project
1 Limited Clinical Realism The virtual environment, while immersive, cannot fully replicate the sensory complexity and psychological pressure of a live obstetric emergency, which may affect the transferability of skills to actual clinical practice. Skills developed in VR may not fully transfer to actual clinical practice, particularly for hands-on procedural tasks requiring physical precision.
2 Narrow Scenario Scope The simulation covers only a single, linear sequence of PPH management and does not expose learners to the full clinical spectrum of PPH presentations or alternative management pathways. Limits the simulation's ability to prepare students for the complete range of obstetric emergencies they may encounter in a real ward setting.
3 Small User Testing Sample User testing was conducted with a limited number of participants, comprising a small group of peers, medical students, and a clinical advisor, rather than a larger and more diverse cohort of qualified clinicians. Reduces the generalisability of usability findings and may not fully reflect the experience of the broader intended target population.
4 Evaluation of Learning Outcomes The project does not include long-term assessments to evaluate knowledge retention, clinical performance, or real-world impact. Without rigorous outcome data, it remains difficult to determine the true educational effectiveness of the simulation.
5 Clinical Consultation Constraints Coordinating with clinical advisors was challenging given the demanding schedules of practising doctors, with sessions frequently rescheduled due to on-call duties and ward commitments. Constrained the frequency and depth of expert feedback at certain development stages, limiting clinical validation at key design decision points.

7.3 Scalability and Future Features

Although the present prototype provides an excellent framework for training procedures individually, there is much room for developing a comprehensive training system. Specifically, implementing a patient avatar that demonstrates different reactions depending on a participant's clinical choices would be one of the most effective enhancements at this point. Such an addition would mean the inclusion of adaptive physiological characteristics of patients, such as vital signs that change as a result of clinical decisions made, vocal signs of pain when performing uterine massage, and visible deterioration resulting from the lack of timely intervention. In addition, multiplayer options should be added, allowing several participants to play their role in the ward and thus receive not only the skills of performing procedures but also training in how to delegate tasks and communicate within a team.

Implementing voice recognition software can also enhance the system through automatic debriefing after a procedure. Specifically, each student would explain what actions have been taken in a simulation, which will be analysed independently by the system to evaluate their recollection of details and communication skills. Finally, the standalone quality of Meta Quest 3 allows for easy scalability of the simulation platform.

7.4 Conclusion

The creation of this VR simulation for postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) management is an essential step in addressing the disparity between medical education and actual clinical practice. The simulation was driven by a clear clinical gap, with PPH being the primary cause of maternal mortality but rarely seen enough for most students to have had firsthand experience with managing it effectively, resulting in students graduating without ever taking charge of a PPH case (Say et al., 2014). Traditional educational methods, such as lectures and manikin-based learning, do not provide sufficient exposure to the stress of an actual situation in the labor ward.

The use of realistic bleeding and declining vitals while making critical decisions on the fly, recreates the environment needed to acquire clinical skills. All scenarios were created in collaboration with clinical experts from the National University Hospital to reflect realistic interactions, from establishing IV access to the proper sequence of uterotonic administration.

Running on a standalone Meta Quest 3 device, the simulation demonstrates that medical training can be provided in a convenient and portable manner at a time and pace that suits the student's schedule. In contrast to a traditional training session in which feedback comes from the instructor, the simulation responds to the student's decisions, enabling self-guided learning and repetition. Overall, the end result is the development of an effective product that is both technologically sound and clinically accurate. Most importantly, the project ensures that a student's initial exposure to a postpartum hemorrhage does not come from an actual patient.
