  1. Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement & Background

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) is one of the most critical obstetric emergencies in modern medicine, defined as blood loss exceeding 500 mL following a vaginal delivery or 1,000 mL following a caesarean section. It remains the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, accounting for approximately 27% of all maternal deaths globally, translating to roughly 14 million cases and 70,000 deaths annually (Say et al., 2014; WHO, 2023). The condition can deteriorate rapidly, with patients losing upwards of 1,000 mL of blood before displaying visible signs of haemodynamic instability, leaving clinicians with an extremely narrow window for effective intervention (Rath, 2021). In Singapore, while maternal mortality rates are significantly lower than global averages, PPH remains a leading cause of maternal morbidity, with severe cases resulting in ICU admissions, emergency hysterectomies, and long-term psychological trauma (Chauke et al., 2023).

Despite its clinical significance, the "high-acuity, low-frequency" nature of PPH means that medical students in Singapore rarely have the opportunity to engage actively in its management during clinical rotations. Because these events are life-threatening and demand immediate senior-level intervention, students are ethically and practically relegated to the role of passive observers. They may witness a PPH resuscitation in the room, but are seldom given the opportunity to lead, or even participate in, the decision-making process. This creates a significant theory-practice gap, where students possess the physiological knowledge of PPH management but lack the environmental exposure required to execute these protocols confidently under intense pressure.

Current training modalities, primarily consisting of didactic lectures and scheduled manikin-based simulations, fail to bridge this gap effectively. Lectures provide theoretical grounding but lack the sensory immersion needed to prepare students for the visual shock of rapid blood loss and the psychological pressure of a deteriorating patient. Manikin-based simulations, while more hands-on, are limited by their high cost, resource intensity, and logistical complexity, meaning students often receive only one or two practice opportunities per academic year. This frequency is far below the threshold required for procedural confidence to become reflexive. Furthermore, traditional group simulations often suffer from the "observer effect," where only one student takes the lead while peers watch passively, resulting in inconsistent skill acquisition across the cohort.

Without sufficient hands-on exposure, students enter their housemanship with theoretical confidence but practical hesitation. When confronted with a real PPH in a labor ward, this hesitation can manifest as fixation errors, delayed escalation, or an inability to prioritise interventions under pressure. Consequently, there is an urgent need for a training solution that provides a high-fidelity, risk-free environment where students can move beyond passive observation and develop the procedural competence and clinical reflexes required to manage a postpartum hemorrhage effectively.

1.2 Novelty and Value Proposition 

To address the training gaps identified in current obstetric education, this project proposes the development of a Virtual Reality (VR) simulation specifically designed for PPH management training. Built on the Meta Quest 3 headset and developed in Unity, the simulation places the learner inside a virtual NUH labor ward where they must independently manage a deteriorating postpartum patient through a structured sequence of clinical interventions. This directly tackles the core problem: that medical students currently have no safe, repeatable, and hands-on platform to practice PPH management before entering the real ward.

The novelty of this solution lies in its ability to transcend the limitations of traditional manikin-based simulation. Manikins are inherently limited by their static nature and high logistical requirements, meaning students receive little more than one or two practice opportunities per year. In contrast, the Meta Quest 3 is portable and standalone, allowing a student to engage in a high-intensity PPH drill multiple times in a single session without any additional infrastructure. This enables the high-frequency repetition of procedural sequences until actions become reflexive, functioning as a "flight simulator" for obstetric emergencies and ensuring that a student's first experience managing a life-threatening bleed is not on a live patient (Ericsson, 2004).

By concentrating development efforts on procedural accuracy and sequential clinical workflow, this simulation provides a robust and scalable bridge between theoretical classroom knowledge and the high-stakes reality of the delivery suite.

1.3 Scope Refinement following Interim Assessment

Following the interim project presentation and subsequent consultations with clinical stakeholders, the scope of this project was significantly refined to reflect the practical constraints of a Final Year Project timeline. The initially proposed version of the project included aspects of environmental realism, empathy training, and voice recognition, to facilitate more natural interactions between a doctor and a patient. While there is significant merit to such a proposal, it quickly became evident after the first iterations that implementing all three elements at once would not be feasible within the timeframe of this project.

In particular, features like voice recognition and empathy-driven interactions would have required much more complex development, such as natural language processing systems and detailed conversational branching. This went beyond what we could reasonably achieve, and pushing ahead with it risked affecting the quality and stability of the core simulation. Because of this, we made a conscious decision to narrow our focus to what mattered most clinically and what we could deliver well ,guiding learners through a clear, structured management protocol for PPH.

Instead of creating a fully open, highly realistic environment with dynamic conversations, the final simulation follows a guided sequence of clinical events. This ensures that learners move through the correct steps in the right order, helping them build a strong, systematic approach to decision-making without the added complexity of open-ended interactions. While this is more constrained than what we initially envisioned, it allowed us to focus our efforts on getting the clinical workflow right, ensuring the system runs reliably, and providing meaningful feedback to users.

This refinement in scope allowed us to  come up with a more streamlined and reliable learning instrument, that prioritises on building core procedural skills and clinical sequencing first, thus creating a solid foundation that future iterations can build on, such as the ones discussed in Section 7.3.

2. Design Strategy

2.2 Identified Training Gaps

Identifying training gaps is one of the most crucial steps in our research study since it deals with why conventional medical training programs cannot equip students with knowledge and skills that would help them handle the emergency situation during active PPH. From our literature review and discussion with Dr. Arundhati Gosavi, we were able to identify two major shortcomings of current educational programs in training students for emergencies involving PPH. First, conventional medical training does not help manage cognitive overload when dealing with real PPH cases. Students need to consider a lot of variables while handling such emergencies  from assessing patients' deteriorating vital signs to remembering drug dosages to manually intervening in a limited period of time. This level of multitasking can never be accomplished through lectures alone or occasional training sessions with a manikin. Cognitive overload is common among young doctors who face their first-ever obstetric emergencies.

Second, tasks that involve motor skills such as obtaining intravenous access, conducting uterine massage, and administering uterotonic drugs need physical practice to develop muscle memory and ensure procedural mastery. However, because formal PPH training is held only twice a year, students lack opportunities to develop the necessary skills.

2.1.2 Clinical Workflow and Scenario Logic

The clinical workflow developed for this simulation is structured to guide the learner through a realistic progression of PPH management, transitioning from routine postpartum monitoring to active crisis response. The scenario is built on a progressive deterioration model, where the learner initially encounters a stable patient before signs of hemorrhage emerge. This gradual escalation mirrors real clinical situations where early warning signs must be recognised promptly. Pre-existing risk factors such as maternal anaemia, delivery of a macrosomic baby, and the use of controlled cord traction are embedded into the patient profile, ensuring learners understand that PPH is often the result of identifiable clinical risks rather than a sudden unexpected event.

Once hemorrhage is initiated, the workflow follows a structured sequence aligned with standard emergency management protocols, progressing through recognition of bleeding, vital sign assessment, IV access, blood investigations, and uterotonic administration. Each stage must be completed before the next can be unlocked, reinforcing systematic and prioritised care. The simulation also incorporates time sensitivity, with physiological indicators such as heart rate and blood pressure changing dynamically as the scenario progresses. Learners are required to perform both procedural tasks and clinical decision-making simultaneously, ensuring the experience addresses technical competence and analytical reasoning in equal measure.

2.4 Development Process and Storyboarding

The development of the VR simulation followed a structured, iterative design cycle that transitioned from identifying human-centric problems to engineering a high-fidelity clinical solution. This process ensured that the final technical product was not merely a digital environment, but a targeted educational intervention designed to address the specific cognitive deficiencies and procedural hesitations of medical students. By focusing on the user experience through a structured persona, the development remained anchored in the reality of the learner’s transition from theoretical knowledge to clinical application.

Phase 1: Persona Development and Problem Scaffolding

The initial stage of development focused on the creation of “John,” a representative Year 4 medical student persona. John served as a critical design tool to visualise the “hidden” gaps in current medical education that are often overlooked in purely technical manuals. Through the development of this persona, we identified that while a student may possess the requisite theoretical knowledge to pass a standardised exam, they often lack the psychological preparedness and "clinical courage" required to lead an active Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) resuscitation. By mapping John’s academic journey, we identified that the “substantial issue” was not a lack of access to medical textbooks, but rather the absence of a safe, low-stakes environment to fail and practice complex decision-making. This persona-driven research highlighted that the VR system needed to prioritise a sense of responsibility and procedural flow to effectively bridge the gap between a student’s classroom confidence and their real-world clinical performance.

With the problem defined through the lens of John’s educational needs, the development shifted toward a detailed technical storyboard. 3. VR System Development [c]

3.1 System Architecture &Technical Overview

The system architecture of the Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) VR Simulation is designed as a modular, event-driven framework optimised for high-fidelity clinical training on the Meta Quest 3 headset. The architecture is bifurcated into three primary layers: the Hardware/Driver Layer, the Middleware/SDK Layer, and the Application Logic Layer, ensuring a clear separation of concerns between the physical input and clinical consequences.

At the Hardware Layer, the system utilises the Quest 3’s advanced sensors for 6 Degrees-of-Freedom (6-DoF) tracking and high-resolution passthrough. This layer communicates with the Middleware Layer via the OpenXR standard. By utilising the Meta XR Core SDK and Interaction SDK, the system translates raw sensor data into actionable "Interactors." This architectural decision allows the simulation to process complex hand-tracking data such as "pinch" and "poke" gestures and map them to virtual medical instruments with sub-millimeter precision.

The Application Logic Layer is governed by a Centralised State Machine designed to handle the non-linear nature of medical emergencies. Unlike traditional linear software, this simulation utilises a Sequence Manager (implemented via the ButtonSequencer class) which leverages C# Asynchronous Coroutines. This allows the architecture to handle time-dependent clinical events such as the 10-second spreading of the StainedSheet or the staggered activation of NurseAudio without freezing the main execution thread. By using IEnumerator functions, the system can "yield" execution, effectively creating a "living" medical environment where vitals (e.g., slowBeat vs. fastBeat) and visual cues change dynamically based on the user's progress.

A critical component of this architecture is the Interaction Wrapper and Event System. By employing the PointableUnityEventWrapper, the system decouples the physical interaction from the logic. When a user interacts with a Syringe, the architecture does not just trigger a static response; it queries a Validation Layer (the TrayItemChecker). This layer compares the user's action against a predefined procedural array (objectHighlighters and placementGuides). If the interaction matches the expected clinical step, the system updates the global state; if not, it triggers a visual "Glow" feedback loop via the GlowObjectCmd system.

Furthermore, the architecture includes a Spatial UI Subsystem. Because medical simulations require constant data monitoring, the system implements a dynamic UI placement logic. Using the SyringeCollisionPopup script, the architecture calculates the contact point between the Syringe and the MotherBody tag, then uses a Billboarding Algorithm to ensure the feedback canvas always faces the user’s MainCamera. This ensures that critical instructional information is never lost in the 3D environment, regardless of the user's physical orientation in the room.

Finally, the data flow is optimised for the mobile chipset of the Quest 3 by utilising the Vulkan Graphics API. This architectural choice supports advanced rendering features like "Optimise Buffer Discards," which maintain a high frame rate (72Hz+) even when the simulation is managing multiple concurrent scripts, 3D audio spatialisation, and dynamic lighting for the emergency room environment. This robust architecture ensures that the simulation remains a reliable, immersive, and pedagogically sound tool for emergency obstetric training.

3.3 VR Scenario Design  

The VR scenario is meticulously structured into five chronological stages that mirror the high-stakes environment of a delivery ward. Each scene is designed to move the learner from passive observation to active clinical intervention, using interactive "quizzes" and "decision points" to ensure that the student's mental processing is aligned with the physical actions required to save a life.

Scene 0: Scenario Introduction (Context)

The simulation commences in a relatively calm environment to establish a baseline. The learner is introduced to the patient, a 35-year-old female who has just completed a normal vaginal delivery. The primary goal of this scene is to test the student's ability to identify "red flags" before they escalate into an emergency.

Interactive Logic: The system presents a "Patient Profile" UI. The learner must review the admission Hb of 9.6 g/dL and the newborn’s weight of 3.9kg.

The Quiz Trigger: A pop-up asks the student to identify if the patient is at high risk for PPH. This is a critical "Mental Processing" check. The clinical reasoning provided in the simulation explains that an Hb < 11 g/dL indicates anemia, which significantly reduces the patient's physiological reserve. By forcing the student to acknowledge these factors (Macrosomia and Anemia) through a "Yes/No" submission, the simulation primes them to "act fast" once the bleeding begins.

Figure 11:  Scene 0 interactive quiz prompt assessing learner identification of PPH risk factors.

Scene 1: Emergency Recognition (Sensory Triggering)

In this scene, the simulation shifts from data review to crisis recognition. The transition is triggered by an auditory alert from the midwife: "Doctor, she's soaked two sheets already!" This is accompanied by the visual rendering of blood spreading on the delivery bed.

The Task: The student must perform a Visual Estimation of Blood Loss (VEBL). In traditional training, blood loss is often underestimated. To combat this, the VR environment requires the student to interact with the blood-soaked linen and submit an estimate.

The Quiz Trigger: A multiple-choice prompt asks the learner to estimate the volume (e.g., 200ml, 500ml, 800ml and 1000ml). Selecting 800ml/ 1000ml is the only way to progress, reinforcing the clinical standard that any loss over 500ml constitutes an emergency. This scene teaches the student to trust clinical signs over subjective observation.

Figure 12:  Scene 1 interactive quiz prompt assessing learner visual estimation of blood loss

Scene 2: Medical Intervention (Intravenous Access)

In this scene, the simulation transitions from the identification of the crisis to the initial phase of medical stabilisation. The urgency of the situation is reinforced by the patient's deteriorating vitals on the monitor, and the student must prioritise establishing a reliable circulatory route. This stage is designed to address the motor skill deficiency in selecting and preparing high-stakes medical equipment under time pressure.

The Task: The student must interact with the medical trolley to select the appropriate Intravenous (IV) equipment for fluid resuscitation. This involves identifying the correct gauge of the cannula required for emergency volume replacement. In a real-world PPH scenario, the speed and bore-size of the IV access can determine the success of subsequent fluid and blood transfusions. The VR environment requires the student to physically pick up the cannula and prepare for insertion.

The Quiz Trigger: A multiple-choice prompt pauses the action and asks the learner: "Which size of IV cannula is most appropriate for a patient experiencing PPH?" with options including 22G, 20G, and 16G/18G. Selecting the 16G or 18G large-bore cannula is the only way to progress. This reinforces the clinical logic that large-bore access is mandatory for rapid resuscitation. The scene teaches the student that procedural precision is just as vital as diagnostic speed during an obstetric emergency.

Figure 13:  Scene 2 interactive quiz prompt assessing learner identification of correct gauge of the cannula required for emergency volume replacement.

Scene 3: Medical Intervention (Blood Test and Laboratory Analysis)

In this scene, the simulation moves from the physical setup of the IV to the essential task of diagnostic quantification. While the IV access allows for the administration of fluids and medications, a formal blood test is required to assess the patient’s current physiological state and prepare for potential deterioration. This stage is designed to teach the student that physical observation must be supported by accurate laboratory data to provide a comprehensive clinical picture.

The Task: The student must utilise the previously established large-bore IV line to draw a series of blood samples. In the VR environment, this requires a specific sequence of actions: selecting the correct color-coded collection tubes from the medical trolley. This reinforces the procedural accuracy required to ensure that the laboratory receives the correct samples for urgent processing, as errors in tube selection can lead to critical delays in a high-stress ward environment.

The Quiz Trigger: A multiple-choice prompt pauses the action and asks the learner: "Which of the following blood tests should be ordered for a patient with PPH?" with options including Full Blood Count (FBC), Group and Cross-match, Coagulation Profile, and Renal/Liver Function tests. Selecting "All of the above" is the only way to progress. This reinforces the clinical protocol that a comprehensive panel is necessary to monitor Hemoglobin levels, prepare for blood transfusions, and check for clotting deficiencies (Thrombin) simultaneously.

Figure 14:  Scene 3 interactive quiz prompt assessing learner identification of correct color-coded collection tubes

Scene 4: Medical Intervention (Uterotonics Administration)

In this scene, the simulation progresses from diagnostic preparation to active hemorrhage control. Following the establishment of IV access and completion of blood investigations, the patient continues to exhibit moderate bleeding with signs of physiological compensation (tachycardia with relatively stable blood pressure). This stage emphasises timely pharmacological intervention alongside physical techniques to promote uterine contraction and reduce ongoing blood loss. It reinforces that postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) management requires both mechanical (uterine massage) and medical (uterotonics) approaches simultaneously.

The Task: The student is first prompted by the nurse to initiate uterine massage, supported by an on-screen animation that the learner must follow. This reinforces immediate first-line management for uterine atony. 3.5 Core Functionalities

This section outlines the core functionalities of the VR-based postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) training system, focusing on three key components: interaction system, decision-making points, and feedback system. These components are designed to simulate real clinical workflows while promoting active learning, critical thinking, and procedural accuracy. By integrating these elements, the system ensures that learners are not passive observers but active participants in patient management.

3.5.1 Interaction System

The interaction system defines how learners physically engage with the virtual clinical environment. Designed using a guided point-and-click approach, it balances usability with realistic hospital workflows:

  • Diagnostic Interactions: Learners interact with the patient and monitors to assess clinical status. Clicking the monitor displays vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure), while interacting with the soaked bedsheet allows learners to estimate blood loss volume.
  • Procedural Interactions: The system simulates real-world clinical tasks, such as selecting the appropriate cannula size and inserting an IV line. The system enforces strict task order, blocking progression until essential steps are completed.
  • Multimodal Tasks: In later scenes, interactions become complex. Learners must perform uterine massage by following guided animations while simultaneously interacting with a medication tray to administer uterotonics.
  • Usability Aids: To manage cognitive load, the system utilises pop-up instructions, allowing the learner to focus on clinical decisions rather than struggling with VR navigation.

3.5.2 Decision-Making Points

Decision-making points are strategically embedded to develop clinical reasoning and prioritisation skills under time-sensitive conditions:

  • Diagnostic Thresholds: Learners must visually estimate blood loss and apply clinical thresholds to officially classify the situation as a PPH, determining the urgency of the intervention.
  • Treatment Selection: Learners must justify their actions, such as selecting a large-bore cannula (16G/18G) for rapid fluid resuscitation, or ordering a comprehensive panel of blood investigations (FBC, coagulation profile, crossmatch).
  • Pharmacological Sequencing: Learners are presented with a tray of uterotonic medications (oxytocin, misoprostol, tranexamic acid, carboprost) and must arrange them in the correct clinical order. Incorrect sequencing prevents progression.
  • Error-Based Learning: Incorrect decisions do not instantly terminate the simulation. Instead, learners are permitted multiple attempts, building confidence through reflective trial-and-error.

3.5.3 Feedback System

The system provides immediate, continuous, and meaningful responses to learner actions, combining visual, verbal, and physiological feedback:

  • Immediate Task Feedback: Correct actions trigger green highlights and positive verbal confirmation from the virtual nurse. Incorrect actions trigger red highlights and corrective audio prompts.
  • Real-Time Physiological Response: As the learner successfully administers treatments, the patient’s condition dynamically improves, visible blood loss reduces, heart rate decreases, and blood pressure stabilises. This establishes a clear cause-and-effect relationship for the student.
  • Immersive Audio Guidance: The virtual nurse simulates clinical teamwork by providing verbal updates on patient status and reminding the learner to act quickly, maintaining a sense of urgency without breaking immersion.
  • Summative Debriefing: Upon successful stabilisation, a confirmation message appears. This is followed by a scaffolding reduction (where guidance fades as the learner progresses) and a final review phase where learners can reflect on their clinical performances.

Lighting was configured to be clinical and bright, establishing a calm baseline environment that shifts in tone as the emergency state is triggered.

The scene includes 3D assets such as a patient model, detailed medical hardware including varied sized cannulas, and specific uterotonic vials on the medication tray. These elements were included not for decorative purposes but to ensure that the procedural interactions within the simulation, such as selecting the correct cannula size or identifying the right medication, are grounded in a recognisable clinical context.

6.1.1 Quantitative Approach

This study adopts a mixed-methods approach, integrating both quantitative and qualitative techniques to evaluate the usability and educational effectiveness of the VR simulation developed for PPH training. This approach enables a comprehensive assessment by combining measurable usability outcomes with in-depth expert insights on clinical and pedagogical relevance.

The quantitative component employs the System Usability Scale (SUS), a widely validated tool used to assess system usability across various domains, including healthcare technologies. The SUS consists of a 10-item questionnaire rated on a 5-point Likert scale, ranging from “Strongly Disagree” (1) to “Strongly Agree” (5). It evaluates key dimensions such as ease of use, learnability, system integration, and user confidence. Example items include “I found the system easy to use,” “I felt confident using the system,” and “I found the various functions in this system were well integrated.”

Note: Please refer to Figure 20 in the Appendix for the detailed SUS Questionnaire.

Participants, comprising 8-10 students, will complete the SUS immediately after interacting with the VR simulation. This sample size is appropriate for pilot usability testing, where the aim is to identify usability trends rather than achieve population-level generalisability. The SUS was selected for its efficiency and ability to generate a single usability score that can be benchmarked against established standards.

SUS scores range from 0 to 100, with 68 representing average usability. Scores above this threshold indicate above-average usability, which is particularly important in VR-based medical training. Poor usability or interface complexity may hinder learning outcomes, especially when the objective is to help students understand the sequence of events in managing a PPH scenario.

Quantitative analysis will involve calculating the mean SUS score, standard deviation, and individual item responses to identify usability strengths and weaknesses. These results will support comparisons across iterations of the prototype and guide improvements.

  3. "Would this simulation be useful for training novice learners?"

These findings go beyond what quantitative measures such as the SUS can capture, providing contextual, clinically grounded guidance that will directly inform the next phase of development.

The integration of quantitative and qualitative methods provides a holistic evaluation of the VR simulation. While the SUS offers objective measures of usability, the expert clinical review provides contextual insight into clinical accuracy and educational relevance. This triangulation strengthens the overall validity of the evaluation.

6.2 Expert Clinical Evaluation

Expert clinical evaluation forms a critical component of validating any medical simulation tool, as it ensures that the experience aligns with real-world clinical practice and meets the standards expected of a professional training environment. To this end, Dr. Arundhati, a practising obstetrician with hands-on expertise in managing postpartum haemorrhage, was engaged to assess the prototype. Her evaluation was conducted through a direct trial of the VR simulation, during which she provided structured feedback grounded in her clinical experience. The findings are analysed below across two domains: strengths and recommendations for future development.

6.2.1 Strengths Identified

Dr. Arundhati responded positively to the overall quality of the simulation. The delivery room environment was praised as very good and realistic, affirming that the spatial layout and clinical equipment are sufficiently representative of an actual obstetric setting which is a foundational requirement for effective immersive training. The clinical scenario was also recognised as elaborate and procedurally accurate, with Dr. Arundhati confirmed that the sequence of events follows the correct order for PPH management, which is the simulation's central educational objective. Minor language refinement within the scenario narration was recommended to sharpen clinical terminology. She further acknowledged the level of development achieved within a short timeframe and expressed a desire to experience the refined version, indicating genuine clinical engagement with the tool.

6.2.2 Areas Requiring Improvement

Scene Realism

Several gaps in character behaviour and emergency escalation were identified and She provided feedback that the realism aspect should be enhanced in this VR simulation in the future.The husband character can be depicted as standing and visibly panicked, reflecting the emotional reality of an obstetric emergency. She also suggested that it would be better for more midwives to be shown entering the room to reflect the multi-disciplinary team response expected in practice.

Pharmacological Completeness

Dr. Arundhati affirmed that the simulation already incorporates key uterotonic agents, and recommended expanding the drug trolley in future iterations to include Syntocinon injection and Ergometrine injection alongside the existing medications. Adding these to the medications which are already present on the trolley would further strengthen the simulation's clinical completeness and reinforce the Four T's management framework for learners.

Blood Volume and Haematological Investigations

Dr. Arundhati suggested that the visible blood at the incident site could be increased in future iterations to further heighten the sense of clinical urgency, noting that a more pronounced visual representation would help convey the severity of a major haemorrhage more effectively to learners. Similarly, she proposed that linking the blood collection step to a group and cross-match investigation would be a valuable addition to future versions of the simulation. While not a strict requirement of the current clinical workflow scope, she felt it would enrich the learning experience by exposing learners to this standard preparatory step in haemorrhage management.

Pictorial Blood Loss Estimation Aid

Dr. Arundhati suggested that a pictorial blood loss estimation chart could be incorporated as a pop-up feedback element following the blood loss estimation question in future iterations. Given that such charts are widely used in clinical practice, she felt this addition would further enrich the learning experience by reinforcing a practical, evidence-based skill directly relevant to PPH assessment.

Usability: Colour Contrast and Audio

Dr. Arundhati offered two usability suggestions for future development. Firstly, she proposed that introducing colour differentiation between question prompts and answer options would improve readability and reduce cognitive load within the visually demanding VR environment. Secondly, she recommended exploring the addition of ambient audio in future versions including clinical alarms, monitor sounds, staff communication, and instructional prompts, noting that this would further enhance immersion and better reflect the auditory complexity of a real obstetric emergency.

6.2.3 Summary

Dr. Arundhati's evaluation affirmed that the simulation has established a strong and credible clinical foundation, with a realistic environment, accurate procedural sequence, and commendable overall execution. Her feedback was largely constructive and forward-looking, offering a set of recommendations to enrich future iterations of the simulation. These include enhancing scene realism through more dynamic character behaviour, expanding the drug trolley to include additional uterotonic agents such as Syntocinon and Ergometrine, and exploring interaction design improvements such as colour contrast, audio integration, and a pictorial blood loss estimation aid. The suggestion to increase blood volume visibility and incorporate a group and cross-match investigation step further reflects her intent to deepen the clinical authenticity of the experience. Collectively, these recommendations provide a clear and well-informed roadmap for the continued development of the simulation.

  1. Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement & Background

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) is one of the most critical obstetric emergencies in modern medicine, defined as blood loss exceeding 500 mL following a vaginal delivery or 1,000 mL following a caesarean section. It remains the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, accounting for approximately 27% of all maternal deaths globally, translating to roughly 14 million cases and 70,000 deaths annually (Say et al., 2014; WHO, 2023). The condition can deteriorate rapidly, with patients losing upwards of 1,000 mL of blood before displaying visible signs of haemodynamic instability, leaving clinicians with an extremely narrow window for effective intervention (Rath, 2021). In Singapore, while maternal mortality rates are significantly lower than global averages, PPH remains a leading cause of maternal morbidity, with severe cases resulting in ICU admissions, emergency hysterectomies, and long-term psychological trauma (Chauke et al., 2023).

Despite its clinical significance, the "high-acuity, low-frequency" nature of PPH means that medical students in Singapore rarely have the opportunity to engage actively in its management during clinical rotations. Because these events are life-threatening and demand immediate senior-level intervention, students are ethically and practically relegated to the role of passive observers. They may witness a PPH resuscitation in the room, but are seldom given the opportunity to lead, or even participate in, the decision-making process. This creates a significant theory-practice gap, where students possess the physiological knowledge of PPH management but lack the environmental exposure required to execute these protocols confidently under intense pressure.

Current training modalities, primarily consisting of didactic lectures and scheduled manikin-based simulations, fail to bridge this gap effectively. Lectures provide theoretical grounding but lack the sensory immersion needed to prepare students for the visual shock of rapid blood loss and the psychological pressure of a deteriorating patient. Manikin-based simulations, while more hands-on, are limited by their high cost, resource intensity, and logistical complexity, meaning students often receive only one or two practice opportunities per academic year. This frequency is far below the threshold required for procedural confidence to become reflexive. Furthermore, traditional group simulations often suffer from the "observer effect," where only one student takes the lead while peers watch passively, resulting in inconsistent skill acquisition across the cohort.

Without sufficient hands-on exposure, students enter their housemanship with theoretical confidence but practical hesitation. When confronted with a real PPH in a labor ward, this hesitation can manifest as fixation errors, delayed escalation, or an inability to prioritise interventions under pressure. Consequently, there is an urgent need for a training solution that provides a high-fidelity, risk-free environment where students can move beyond passive observation and develop the procedural competence and clinical reflexes required to manage a postpartum hemorrhage effectively.

1.2 Novelty and Value Proposition

To address the training gaps identified in current obstetric education, this project proposes the development of a Virtual Reality (VR) simulation specifically designed for PPH management training. Built on the Meta Quest 3 headset and developed in Unity, the simulation places the learner inside a virtual NUH labor ward where they must independently manage a deteriorating postpartum patient through a structured sequence of clinical interventions. This directly tackles the core problem: that medical students currently have no safe, repeatable, and hands-on platform to practice PPH management before entering the real ward.

The novelty of this solution lies in its ability to transcend the limitations of traditional manikin-based simulation. Manikins are inherently limited by their static nature and high logistical requirements, meaning students receive little more than one or two practice opportunities per year. In contrast, the Meta Quest 3 is portable and standalone, allowing a student to engage in a high-intensity PPH drill multiple times in a single session without any additional infrastructure. This enables the high-frequency repetition of procedural sequences until actions become reflexive, functioning as a "flight simulator" for obstetric emergencies and ensuring that a student's first experience managing a life-threatening bleed is not on a live patient (Ericsson, 2004).

By concentrating development efforts on procedural accuracy and sequential clinical workflow, this simulation provides a robust and scalable bridge between theoretical classroom knowledge and the high-stakes reality of the delivery suite.

3. VR System Development

3.1 System Architecture &Technical Overview

The system architecture of the Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) VR Simulation is designed as a modular, event-driven framework optimised for high-fidelity clinical training on the Meta Quest 3 headset. The architecture is bifurcated into three primary layers: the Hardware/Driver Layer, the Middleware/SDK Layer, and the Application Logic Layer, ensuring a clear separation of concerns between the physical input and clinical consequences.

At the Hardware Layer, the system utilises the Quest 3’s advanced sensors for 6 Degrees-of-Freedom (6-DoF) tracking and high-resolution passthrough. This layer communicates with the Middleware Layer via the OpenXR standard. By utilising the Meta XR Core SDK and Interaction SDK, the system translates raw sensor data into actionable "Interactors." This architectural decision allows the simulation to process complex hand-tracking data such as "pinch" and "poke" gestures and map them to virtual medical instruments with sub-millimeter precision.

The Application Logic Layer is governed by a Centralised State Machine designed to handle the non-linear nature of medical emergencies. Unlike traditional linear software, this simulation utilises a Sequence Manager (implemented via the ButtonSequencer class) which leverages C# Asynchronous Coroutines. This allows the architecture to handle time-dependent clinical events such as the 10-second spreading of the StainedSheet or the staggered activation of NurseAudio without freezing the main execution thread. By using IEnumerator functions, the system can "yield" execution, effectively creating a "living" medical environment where vitals (e.g., slowBeat vs. fastBeat) and visual cues change dynamically based on the user's progress.

A critical component of this architecture is the Interaction Wrapper and Event System. By employing the PointableUnityEventWrapper, the system decouples the physical interaction from the logic. When a user interacts with a Syringe, the architecture does not just trigger a static response; it queries a Validation Layer (the TrayItemChecker). This layer compares the user's action against a predefined procedural array (objectHighlighters and placementGuides). If the interaction matches the expected clinical step, the system updates the global state; if not, it triggers a visual "Glow" feedback loop via the GlowObjectCmd system.

Furthermore, the architecture includes a Spatial UI Subsystem. Because medical simulations require constant data monitoring, the system implements a dynamic UI placement logic. Using the SyringeCollisionPopup script, the architecture calculates the contact point between the Syringe and the MotherBody tag, then uses a Billboarding Algorithm to ensure the feedback canvas always faces the user’s MainCamera. This ensures that critical instructional information is never lost in the 3D environment, regardless of the user's physical orientation in the room.

Finally, the data flow is optimised for the mobile chipset of the Quest 3 by utilising the Vulkan Graphics API. This architectural choice supports advanced rendering features like "Optimise Buffer Discards," which maintain a high frame rate (72Hz+) even when the simulation is managing multiple concurrent scripts, 3D audio spatialisation, and dynamic lighting for the emergency room environment. This robust architecture ensures that the simulation remains a reliable, immersive, and pedagogically sound tool for emergency obstetric training.

3.5 Core Functionalities

This section outlines the core functionalities of the VR-based postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) training system, focusing on three key components: interaction system, decision-making points, and feedback system. These components are designed to simulate real clinical workflows while promoting active learning, critical thinking, and procedural accuracy. By integrating these elements, the system ensures that learners are not passive observers but active participants in patient management.

3.5.1 Interaction System

The interaction system defines how learners physically engage with the virtual clinical environment. Designed using a guided point-and-click approach, it balances usability with realistic hospital workflows:

  • Diagnostic Interactions: Learners interact with the patient and monitors to assess clinical status. Clicking the monitor displays vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure), while interacting with the soaked bedsheet allows learners to estimate blood loss volume.
  • Procedural Interactions: The system simulates real-world clinical tasks, such as selecting the appropriate cannula size and inserting an IV line. The system enforces strict task order, blocking progression until essential steps are completed.
  • Multimodal Tasks: In later scenes, interactions become complex. Learners must perform uterine massage by following guided animations while simultaneously interacting with a medication tray to administer uterotonics.
  • Usability Aids: To manage cognitive load, the system utilises pop-up instructions, allowing the learner to focus on clinical decisions rather than struggling with VR navigation.

3.5.2 Decision-Making Points

Decision-making points are strategically embedded to develop clinical reasoning and prioritisation skills under time-sensitive conditions:

  • Diagnostic Thresholds: Learners must visually estimate blood loss and apply clinical thresholds to officially classify the situation as a PPH, determining the urgency of the intervention.
  • Treatment Selection: Learners must justify their actions, such as selecting a large-bore cannula (16G/18G) for rapid fluid resuscitation, or ordering a comprehensive panel of blood investigations (e.g., FBC, coagulation profile, crossmatch).
  • Pharmacological Sequencing: Learners are presented with a tray of uterotonic medications (oxytocin, misoprostol, tranexamic acid, carboprost) and must arrange them in the correct clinical order. Incorrect sequencing prevents progression.
  • Error-Based Learning: Incorrect decisions do not instantly terminate the simulation. Instead, learners are permitted multiple attempts, building confidence through reflective trial-and-error.

3.5.3 Feedback System

The system provides immediate, continuous, and meaningful responses to learner actions, combining visual, verbal, and physiological feedback:

  • Immediate Task Feedback: Correct actions trigger green highlights and positive verbal confirmation from the virtual nurse. Incorrect actions trigger red highlights and corrective audio prompts.

  • Immersive Audio Guidance: The virtual nurse simulates clinical teamwork by providing verbal updates on patient status and reminding the learner to act quickly, maintaining a sense of urgency without breaking immersion.
  • Summative Debriefing: Upon successful stabilisation, a confirmation message appears. This is followed by a scaffolding reduction (where guidance fades as the learner progresses) and a final review phase where learners can reflect on their clinical performances.

6.1.1 Quantitative Approach

This study adopts a mixed-methods approach, integrating both quantitative and qualitative techniques to evaluate the usability and educational effectiveness of the VR simulation developed for PPH training. The quantitative component employs the System Usability Scale (SUS), a widely validated tool used to assess system usability across various domains, including healthcare technologies. The SUS consists of a 10-item questionnaire rated on a 5-point Likert scale, ranging from “Strongly Disagree” (1) to “Strongly Agree” (5). It evaluates key dimensions such as ease of use, learnability, system integration, and user confidence. Example items include “I found the system easy to use,” “I felt confident using the system,” and “I found the various functions in this system were well integrated.”

Participants, comprising 8-10 students, will complete the SUS immediately after interacting with the VR simulation. The SUS was selected for its efficiency and ability to generate a single usability score that can be benchmarked against established standards.

SUS scores range from 0 to 100, with 68 representing average usability. Scores above this threshold indicate above-average usability, which is particularly important in VR-based medical training. Poor usability or interface complexity may hinder learning outcomes, especially when the objective is to help students understand the sequence of events in managing a PPH scenario.

Quantitative analysis will involve calculating the mean SUS score, standard deviation, and individual item responses to identify usability strengths and weaknesses. These results will support comparisons across iterations of the prototype and guide improvements.

7.3 Scalability and Future Features

While the current prototype establishes a strong foundation for individual procedural training, there is significant potential to expand the platform into a more comprehensive training ecosystem. The most impactful near-term addition would be the integration of AI-powered patient avatars that exhibit adaptive responses based on the learner's clinical decisions, including vital signs that fluctuate in real-time, verbal pain expressions during uterine massage, and visible deterioration in response to delayed intervention. Alongside this, multiplayer functionality would allow multiple students to occupy the same virtual ward, each assigned a distinct clinical role, thereby training not only procedural competence but also communication and task delegation under pressure better reflecting the team-based reality of a real obstetric emergency.

Voice recognition technology could further extend the platform's utility by enabling automated post-simulation debriefing, where students verbally recount the steps they performed and the system automatically scores recall accuracy and communication clarity without requiring a human facilitator. Looking further ahead, the standalone nature of the Meta Quest 3 headset means the platform is inherently scalable across institutions. Expanding the scenario library to cover a broader range of obstetric emergencies would make the simulation applicable not just to medical students but to midwifery trainees and junior doctors across diverse healthcare settings globally.

Although the present prototype provides an excellent framework for training procedures individually, there is much room for developing a comprehensive training system. Specifically, implementing a patient avatar that demonstrates different reactions depending on a participant's clinical choices would be one of the most effective enhancements at this point. Such an addition would mean the inclusion of adaptive physiological characteristics of patients, such as vital signs that change as a result of clinical decisions made, vocal signs of pain when performing uterine massage, and visible deterioration resulting from the lack of timely intervention. In addition, multiplayer options should be added, allowing several participants to play their role in the ward and thus receive not only the skills of performing procedures but also training in how to delegate tasks and communicate within a team. Implementing voice recognition software can also enhance the system through automatic debriefing after a procedure. Specifically, each student would explain what actions have been taken in a simulation, which will be analyzed independently by the system to evaluate their recollection of details and communication skills. Finally, the standalone quality of Meta Quest 3 allows for easy scalability of the simulation platform.

Traditional modalities such as lectures and manikin sessions fail to replicate the sensory pressure of a real labor ward crisis. By rendering active blood loss, triggering deteriorating vitals, and requiring sequential time-pressured decisions, this simulation creates the conditions under which genuine clinical competence is built. The creation of this VR simulation for postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) management is an essential step in addressing the disparity between medical education and actual clinical practice. The simulation arose due to a gap in the current practices, with PPH being the primary cause of maternal mortality but rarely seen enough for most students to have had firsthand experience with managing it effectively, resulting in students graduating without ever taking charge of a PPH case (Say et al., 2014). The conventional methods of education, such as lectures and manikin-based learning, cannot provide sufficient exposure to the stress of an actual situation in the labor ward. The use of realistic bleeding and declining vitals while making critical decisions on the fly can recreate the environment needed to acquire clinical skills. All scenarios were created in collaboration with clinical experts from the National University Hospital to reflect realistic interactions, from establishing IV access to the proper sequence of uterotonic administration.

Running on a standalone Meta Quest 3 headset, the simulation shows that high-quality medical training can be delivered in a portable, accessible format that fits into a student's own schedule and learning pace. Rather than relying on an instructor to provide feedback, the simulation responds directly to the student's actions, allowing them to learn independently and repeat the scenario as many times as needed. Ultimately, this project has produced a tool that is technically reliable, clinically accurate, and educationally meaningful, ensuring that a student's first experience managing a postpartum hemorrhage is not on a live patient.

When run from a standalone Meta Quest 3 device, the simulation demonstrates that medical training can be provided in a convenient and portable manner at a time and pace that suits the student's schedule. In contrast to a traditional training session in which feedback comes from the instructor, the simulation responds to the student's decisions, enabling self-guided learning and repetition. Overall, the end result is the development of an effective product that is both technologically sound and clinically accurate. 5.4.1  Strengths Identified

Environmental Realism and Clinical Authenticity

Dr. Arun's most prominent commendation was directed at the visual fidelity of the simulated delivery room environment, which she described as very good and realistic. This indicates that the spatial layout, clinical equipment, and overall atmosphere are sufficiently representative of an actual obstetric setting. Environmental realism is a foundational requirement for immersive medical simulation, as learners must feel situated in a plausible clinical context for procedural learning to transfer effectively to real-world practice.

Clinical Scenario Design and Procedural Accuracy

The clinical scenario was recognised as elaborate and well-constructed, with Dr. Arun confirming that the sequence of clinical events follows the correct order for PPH management. This is a significant validation, as procedural fidelity is the central educational objective of the simulation. Some language refinement within the scenario narration and prompts was recommended to ensure that clinical terminology is precise and appropriate for a professional training audience.

Overall Execution and Engagement

Dr. Arun expressed genuine enthusiasm for the experience, acknowledging that the level of development was commendable given the short timeframe, and indicating that she would personally want to experience the simulation in its refined form. This response signals that the prototype has generated sufficient clinical engagement to motivate continued use, which is a meaningful indicator of a training tool's real-world potential. The typographic design was also noted as acceptable, confirming that font choices do not interfere with readability within the VR environment.

 

5.4.2  Areas Requiring Improvement

Scene Realism: Character Behaviour and Emergency Response

Dr. Arun identified several gaps in the behavioural realism of characters within the scene. The husband character was noted as needing to be shown standing and visibly panicked, rather than remaining static. In a real obstetric emergency, the emotional response of a patient's family member is a genuine part of the clinical atmosphere and contributes meaningfully to the psychological pressure that healthcare professionals must learn to manage. The absence of this element reduces the emotional authenticity of the scenario.

Additionally, Dr. Arun recommended that more midwives should be depicted entering the room in response to the emergency, reflecting the multi-disciplinary team response that characterises real-world PPH management. Most critically, she identified the absence of a Code Pink activation as a significant clinical omission. Code Pink is the standard obstetric haemorrhage emergency code used in hospital settings, and its triggering upon formal PPH diagnosis is a mandated step in the escalation protocol. Including this in the simulation is essential both for procedural accuracy and for training learners in the correct emergency response pathway.

Equipment Accuracy: IV Cannula

A specific and clinically important error was flagged regarding the IV cannula modelled in the simulation. Dr. Arun noted that the current representation — described as resembling strings and a hypodermic needle — does not accurately depict the correct instrument. A No. 16 gauge cannula is the appropriate size for large-bore IV access in PPH management, and learners must be able to recognise and associate this specific device with the correct clinical indication. Inaccurate equipment representations risk embedding incorrect procedural knowledge and undermine the simulation's credibility as a training tool.

Pharmacological Completeness: Uterotonic Drug Trolley

Dr. Arun identified the incomplete pharmacological setup as one of the most critical areas for improvement, emphasising that uterotonic drug administration represents the primary pharmacological learning objective of this simulation. She specified that the following agents must be visibly present on the drug trolley and accessible for use within the scenario:

•           Syntocinon injection

•           Ergometrine injection

•           Tranexamic acid injection 1 g

•           Carboprost injection

•           Misoprostol (Cytotec) 200 mcg — 4 tablets

 

Each of these drugs corresponds to a distinct clinical indication within the Four T's management framework, and learners must be able to identify, select, and administer the correct agent at the appropriate stage of the scenario. Without these medications on the trolley, the simulation cannot fulfil its stated pharmacological training objective, and a central component of the learning experience is effectively absent.

Blood Volume and Haematological Investigations

Two related observations were raised regarding the blood component of the simulation. First, Dr. Arun recommended that the volume of blood visible at the incident site should be significantly increased. The current level does not adequately convey the clinical severity of a major postpartum haemorrhage, and a more pronounced visual representation is necessary to communicate the urgency of the situation to the learner. Second, she noted that the blood collected during the scenario should be linked to a group and cross-match investigation, which is a standard and time-critical early step in preparing for potential blood transfusion in significant haemorrhage. The absence of this step represents a procedural gap that must be addressed to ensure the simulation reflects complete clinical practice.

Pictorial Blood Loss Estimation Aid

Following the in-simulation question on estimating blood loss, Dr. Arun recommended that a pictorial reference chart should appear as part of the answer feedback. Visual blood loss estimation charts are a standard clinical reference tool, enabling practitioners to systematically compare observed blood volumes against illustrated benchmarks. Integrating this as a contextual pop-up within the simulation would reinforce a practical, evidence-based skill, align the experience with real clinical decision-support resources, and provide a more informative and educational response to one of the scenario's key assessment questions.

Colour Differentiation for Questions and Answers

From a usability perspective, Dr. Arun observed that the current interface lacks sufficient colour contrast between question prompts and answer options. Within the visually complex VR environment, clear visual differentiation between these elements is essential for readability and cognitive efficiency. Without distinct colour coding, learners may struggle to quickly distinguish the question from the available responses, increasing unnecessary cognitive load and potentially disrupting engagement during time-sensitive moments in the scenario. This is an interaction design issue that directly affects the usability and accessibility of the simulation's assessment components.

Audio Design: Chaos and Clinical Instruction

Dr. Arun highlighted the absence of meaningful audio as a significant gap in the current prototype. She recommended the inclusion of audio that conveys both the chaotic nature of an obstetric emergency and clear clinical instructions — reflecting the dual auditory environment that healthcare professionals must navigate in a real PPH situation. Relevant audio elements would include medical equipment alarms, patient monitor beeps, verbal staff communication, and patient distress sounds. These components would substantially enhance the sense of urgency, emotional realism, and clinical immersion, and directly address one of the simulation's stated design requirements for dynamic audio integration.

Physical Stability and Headset Handling

These objectives shaped a series of four iterative prototypes, each targeting a distinct technical problem.

 

Figure 5.1 – Interim prototype development objectives, covering five core areas of the VR simulation

 

Prototype 1 & 2 – The Quiz and AI Animation Engine

The first two prototypes addressed two foundational interaction challenges: how to prompt the user's clinical knowledge through an in-simulation assessment, and how to build a believable, scriptable character animation system.

Prototype 1 involved constructing a simple UI quiz within the Scene1Manager script, establishing the framework for interactive decision prompts in VR. The FinalSequence() function was validated as a mechanism to hand off control between scenes, confirming that complex interactive UI could be built within the Unity environment.

Prototype 2 extended this by combining two Unity systems: NavMeshAgent for AI pathfinding which bakes the floor geometry into a navigable map  and an Animator-driven system where C# scripts flip boolean "switches" (isWalking, isTalking) to trigger character animations. This served as a successful proof-of-concept for creating a controllable, believable clinical character.

 

Figure 5.2 – Prototype 1 & 2: Quiz interaction and AI animation engine built using Unity's NavMeshAgent and Animator systems

 

Prototype 3 – Solving for Event Sequencing

A critical challenge identified early in development was ensuring that clinical events — such as patient dialogue and vital sign updates — occurred in the correct sequence and at the correct time. Prototype 3 addressed this through the introduction of a "Director" script (Scene2Manager), a centralised C# controller that governs all other scene objects.

Two core mechanisms were implemented. First, Trigger-based detection: the patient avatar was assigned a Sphere Collider set to "Is Trigger," so that when the player enters proximity, a function in the Director is called to initiate the sequence. Second, Coroutine-based timing: the Director uses a Coroutine with the yield return new WaitForSeconds() command, pausing script execution for a duration equal to the length of the patient's audio line before triggering the next event (e.g., monitor update). This prototype confirmed that complex, timed, player-reactive scenarios could be reliably constructed within the Unity engine.

 

Figure 5.3 – Prototype 3: Event sequencing via the "Director" script, using Sphere Collider triggers and Coroutine-based timing

 

Prototype 4 – Solving for Realism and Empathy

The fourth prototype focused on increasing sensory immersion and testing the feasibility of unscripted patient interaction. Three components were developed in parallel.

Visual Realism (Blood): A "sheet swap" method was implemented as a first proof-of-concept for simulating postpartum bleeding, with animated blood effects identified as the logical next step to increase visual fidelity.

Audio Realism (11Labs): Two audio approaches were compared — real-world clinical audio sourced from YouTube and synthetic voice audio generated via ElevenLabs. The comparison aimed to identify which source produced a more clinically immersive experience.

Empathy (Conversational AI): A conversational AI interface was prototyped using Google Gemini, demonstrating that unscripted, free-form patient conversations were technically feasible. This was identified as a key mechanism for training doctors' communication and empathy skills.

5.2.2  Scope Refinement: From Broad Concept to Focused Clinical Tool

Following the interim project presentation and subsequent consultations with clinical stakeholders, the scope of the project was strategically refined to prioritise technical and procedural mastery over the previously proposed empathy and communication components. Attempting to manage both domains in parallel threatened to dilute the technical rigour required to simulate life-threatening medical interactions with the precision necessary for effective training. The empathy and conversational AI components, while promising as demonstrated in Prototype 4, introduced a level of complexity that could not be adequately validated or refined within the remaining development window.

Consequently, the project focus was narrowed to ensure that engineering efforts were dedicated exclusively to the high-fidelity execution of the "Four T's" management protocol — Tone, Tissue, Trauma, and Thrombin. This refinement enabled deeper concentration on three critical areas:

•           Accuracy of procedural flow and clinical task sequencing

•           Physiological feedback loops reflecting realistic patient deterioration and recovery

•           Rapid diagnostic decision-making under simulated time pressure

By narrowing the project's boundaries, the resulting VR simulation was positioned as a more robust and specialised clinical training tool, designed to bridge the theory-practice gap in emergency obstetric care through a concentrated focus on life-saving technical intervention.

5.2.3  Refined Final Prototype: A Focused Clinical VR Experience

The refined final prototype represents a consolidation of the technical learnings from all four initial prototypes, re-scoped to deliver a coherent, clinically accurate PPH management simulation. The following key improvements were implemented in response to the gaps and feedback identified during the interim phase.

Clearer Instructional Design and User Guidance

A recurring observation from interim testing was that learners required more explicit in-simulation guidance to understand what actions were expected of them. In the refined prototype, on-screen prompts were redesigned to be task-specific and time-appropriate, appearing only when contextually relevant to avoid cognitive overload. Visual cues such as object highlights and directional indicators were added to guide the learner's attention to interactive elements without disrupting immersion.

Improved Procedural Flow and Task Logic

The Director script architecture established in Prototype 3 was significantly expanded to govern the complete PPH management sequence. The refined prototype enforces the Four T's protocol as a structured, gated workflow — each clinical task must be completed correctly before the scenario advances. This ensures that learners cannot bypass critical steps, such as establishing IV access prior to administering uterotonics, reinforcing procedural memory through enforced sequencing.

Enhanced Patient Physiological Feedback

The dynamic patient response system was refined to provide more granular and clinically meaningful feedback. Vital signs — including heart rate, blood pressure, and bleeding severity indicators — now update progressively in response to the learner's actions, with deterioration occurring if key interventions are delayed and stabilisation occurring when correct steps are taken in sequence. This creates a direct and visible consequence loop that strengthens experiential learning.

Focused Audio-Visual Environment

Drawing on the audio comparison findings from Prototype 4, the refined prototype adopted synthetic voice audio (ElevenLabs) for patient dialogue, as this provided greater control over tone, pacing, and clinical scripting. The blood simulation was also refined beyond the initial sheet-swap method, incorporating more dynamic visual representations of haemorrhage severity to provide clearer feedback on patient status. Together, these enhancements increased the overall realism and clinical fidelity of the simulation environment without introducing the complexity of the removed empathy module.

 

The iterative development process — from broad multi-domain prototyping through to a focused, technically rigorous final prototype — demonstrates a disciplined design approach that balances ambition with feasibility. The scope refinement, while a reduction in breadth, ultimately resulted in a deeper and more validated simulation experience aligned with the project's core educational objective: training healthcare professionals in the life-saving management of postpartum haemorrhage.

  2. UI Refinement: The divergence in scores for Q3 (Ease of Use) suggests that the visual prompts for drugs and blood loss estimation need the "pop-up" aids and colour differentiation suggested by the expert.
  3. Clinical Depth: The high usability score validates that the platform is ready to handle the more complex pharmacological additions (Syntocinon/Ergometrine) and haematological steps suggested by Dr. Arun without overwhelming the user.
